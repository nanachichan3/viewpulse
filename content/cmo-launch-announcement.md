## 2. Reddit — "Coming Soon" Announcement

### Subreddit: r/youtube

**Title:**
> YouTube Studio shows you views. Viewpulse shows you why. Built a privacy-first analytics dashboard — launching soon.

**Body:**

Hey r/youtube — I built a tool I've wanted for myself for years.

**The problem:** YouTube Studio tells you what happened. It doesn't tell you *why*.

If you've ever stared at your analytics wondering why Video A killed it while Video B with similar views tanked — you know exactly what I'm talking about. The data YouTube gives you is surface-level. The interesting patterns are buried or just... not there.

I'm building **Viewpulse** — a YouTube analytics dashboard that surfaces what Studio hides.

**What it shows you:**
- CTR breakdown by traffic source (organic vs. suggested vs. browse)
- Avg view duration by video type and publish window
- Subscriber delta trends per video — who's sticking vs. who bounced
- Watch time patterns that explain retention, not just watch time
- Audience retention curves with annotated drop-off points

**The privacy thing:** No data collection. No Google OAuth. Everything processes in your browser. Your analytics stay yours.

Currently in final build. Launching at **viewpulse.rachkovan.com** soon.

**AMA — happy to discuss the build process, the data, or what I've learned analyzing my own channel.**

### Subreddit: r/contentcreator

**Title:**
> Hot take: "post at optimal times" is useless advice. Here's what you should track instead.

**Body:**

Quick question: How many times have you read "post at 3 PM on Fridays" and then wondered why it didn't move the needle for you?

That's not a failure of the advice. It's the premise. Generic posting time recommendations assume your audience is everyone. It isn't.

I analyzed 50 mid-sized creator channels for a project I'm working on (an analytics dashboard called **Viewpulse**). The channels that grew fastest weren't posting at "optimal times" — they were posting when *their specific audience* was active.

**The "optimal posting time" myth breaks down like this:**
- Every niche has a different audience
- Every audience has a different scroll pattern
- "3 PM on Fridays" is averaged across millions of people who don't watch your content

What matters isn't generic best-posting-time advice. What matters is YOUR audience's pattern.

**Three metrics that actually tell you something useful:**

1. **Click-through rate by traffic source** — Organic search CTR tells you if your thumbnail hooks search intent. Suggested CTR tells you if YouTube is nudging your video. Low suggested CTR but high search CTR = your thumbnail is the problem, not the content.

2. **Average view duration by video type** — 50% retention on a 10-minute video is 5 min watch time. 40% retention on a 20-minute video is 8 min. Context changes what "good" means.

3. **Audience retention curves** — A cliff at 0:30 means your hook is weak. A steady decline means your pacing drags. A spike mid-video means something resonated — what was it?

**Built Viewpulse** to surface exactly this. Coming soon → [viewpulse.rachkovan.com](https://viewpulse.rachkovan.com). No data collection. No Google OAuth. Just your data.

Would love to hear: What analytics surprise have you had that changed how you approach content?
